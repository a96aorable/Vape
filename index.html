Vaping vs Smoking: The Facts

Vaping and smoking both involve inhaling substances that can affect health, but they differ in delivery, composition, and risks. Here’s a breakdown comparing the two and addressing why vaping is sometimes considered worse than smoking, based on available evidence.

Mechanism and Substances:

Smoking: Involves burning tobacco, producing smoke with thousands of chemicals, including tar, carbon monoxide, and known carcinogens like benzene and formaldehyde.

Vaping: Uses an electronic device to heat a liquid (e-liquid) containing nicotine, flavorings, and other chemicals (e.g., propylene glycol, vegetable glycerin) into an aerosol. No combustion occurs, reducing some harmful byproducts.

Health Risks:

Smoking: Well-established cause of lung cancer, heart disease, stroke, and COPD. Contains over 7,000 chemicals, with at least 70 known carcinogens. Secondhand smoke poses risks to non-smokers.

Vaping: Fewer harmful chemicals than cigarette smoke, but not risk-free. Long-term effects are less studied due to vaping’s recent emergence. Associated with lung injuries like EVALI and contains heavy metals and volatile compounds.

Nicotine Delivery:

Both deliver addictive nicotine. Vapes can sometimes deliver higher nicotine concentrations than cigarettes, increasing addiction risk, especially with pod-based systems.

Regulation and Safety:

Smoking: Heavily regulated with strong warnings.

Vaping: Less regulated in many places, leading to variable product quality and safety. Illicit products can be dangerous.

Why Vaping Might Be Less Harmful:

It eliminates tar and carbon monoxide and reduces toxicants significantly—Public Health England (2015) estimated vaping is 95% less harmful than smoking. It may serve as a harm-reduction tool for smokers.

Conclusion:

Vaping may be less harmful for existing smokers but is not risk-free. It presents unique risks, particularly for youth and non-smokers. Full cessation from nicotine remains the healthiest goal.
